What it is: Most of the cards you get in Hearthstone come in these packs of 5. You can purchase them directly, or get them as rewards.

Why it’s good: Whenever you are creating a system based off of microtransactions, there’s always the question of “What is the user buying?” Whenever I’ve played a game that requires me to pay just to play it, I respond colorfully and delete it. (I’m looking at you Clash of Clans)

Hearthstone is a card game, so how do you make a digital card that has worth? By wrapping the card acquisition in a little fanfare, Hearthstone adds a level of anticipation and reverence to each pack. Not only that, the pack bursting apart and each card flipping as you click it reinforces Hearthstone’s physical interface.

The blue aura that you see around the last card I open indicates it’s of higher rarity, and the game’s narrator (The Innkeeper) exclaims “Rare!” when I reveal it. It does this with any card that isn’t a common card, so it is guaranteed once a pack. The anticipation is small, but as an avid player of the game, my heart rate skyrockets when I hover on a card with an orange glow. (Which is the rarest type, legendary) It’s one of those hooks that doesn’t lose its magic when you see behind the curtain.

If you want more information on Hearthstone’s physical interface (Inculuding the pack opening), I highly recommend this amazing presentation by one of its designers, Derek Sakamoto.
